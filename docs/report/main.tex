\section{Career Orientation and Related Fields}

\textbf{Definition.} Career orientation is the process of helping individuals understand their interests, personality, strengths, and values and connect that self-understanding to suitable educational and career paths (OECD, 2004; UNESCO-UNEVOC). This approach can be traced back to Frank Parsons (1909), who framed sound career choice as a three-step process: (1) understanding oneself, (2) understanding the world of work, and (3) reasoning about the relationship between the two.

\textbf{General steps in career orientation.} In practice, this process is usually divided into four stages that a human counselor or digital platform should support:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Self-assessment} — understanding one's interests, personality, strengths, and values. This psychology-based stage draws on frameworks such as RIASEC and SCCT.
    \item \textbf{Exploration} — actively investigating potentially suitable educational and career options, ideally through practical exposure rather than only reading abstract job descriptions.
    \item \textbf{Decision-making} — comparing self-assessment and exploration results with real-world information, such as labor demand, admission requirements, and salary levels, to narrow the available options.
    \item \textbf{Planning and review} — turning a decision into a concrete action plan, such as selecting a subject combination or target university, and reviewing it periodically. In Super's theory, a person's self-concept and circumstances continue to evolve.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Related fields to review:}

\begin{enumerate}[a.]
    \item \textbf{Educational and career psychology.} This field explains \textit{how} a person's characteristics---including interests, personality, cognitive strengths, self-efficacy, and values---relate to career fit. It provides the theoretical basis for the platform's self-assessment engine, drawing mainly on Holland's RIASEC model (person--environment fit), Social Cognitive Career Theory (self-efficacy and outcome expectations), Vietnam's "Career Tree" framework, and Super's Life-Span theory. Because an adolescent's self-concept is still evolving, assessment results should be treated as an exploratory snapshot rather than a fixed label.
    \item \textbf{Labor market information systems (LMIS).} This field examines how salary, labor-demand, and industry-growth data are collected, structured, and published. In Vietnam, relevant information is provided by government labor agencies and the General Statistics Office. LMIS supports the platform's market-analytics module and connects to SCCT's concept of outcome expectations: reliable market data allows students to compare their expectations with evidence rather than relying on rumor.
    \item \textbf{HCI and EdTech design for teenagers.} This field examines how technology should be designed specifically for adolescent users and is sometimes called Teen--Computer Interaction (TeenCI). Teenagers are a diverse, dynamic, and technologically aware user group; therefore, interfaces should reflect their developmental stage rather than simply reuse adult-oriented designs. The way a 16-year-old interprets a question, maintains motivation, or develops trust in a recommendation may differ from that of an adult. Consequently, the wording and pacing of an assessment are psychological design choices as well as user-interface choices.
    \item \textbf{Recommender systems.} This computer-science field develops methods for suggesting suitable options from user data. In this project, such methods are applied to careers and university majors. Knowledge-based and hybrid recommenders are particularly relevant to career guidance, although combining personal characteristics from field (a) with current labor-market information from field (b) remains challenging. The project's Visual Pathway \& Market Analytics module is intended to address this gap.
    \item \textbf{Vietnam's education policy framework.} The GDPT 2018 curriculum, the tổ hợp môn (subject-combination) system, and university-admission processes form the institutional context within which the platform must operate. A student's psychological profile becomes actionable only when it is mapped to actual subject combinations, majors, and admission options.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{The common thread.} Psychology is not separate from the other related fields; it is integrated with all of them. LMIS grounds outcome expectations in evidence, TeenCI applies psychology to interface design, recommender systems combine psychological and market data, and Vietnam's policy framework turns a psychological profile into an actionable educational or career decision.

\section{Problems in career orientation}

This section outlines the problems that career orientation must address. These include evidence of student--major mismatch and skills gaps in Vietnam, as well as broader limitations involving assessment validity, labor-market uncertainty, limited resources, adolescent development, family pressure, inequitable access, and algorithmic risk.

\subsection{Market-level problems in Vietnam}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Recent THPT examination cohorts have included approximately 1.07--1.17 million students per year, while approximately 65--68\% have pursued university education. Consequently, guidance focused exclusively on university admission may not serve a substantial proportion of students.
    \item Widely cited, press-reported FALMI estimates suggest that approximately 60\% of students choose an unsuitable major and that approximately 75\% do not adequately understand their chosen field. These figures can be traced to statements reported in 2013 and 2019 rather than to a formally published study and should therefore be interpreted cautiously.
    \item A 2026 survey attributed to Đại Nam University and Soha ($n \approx 9{,}200$ across 10 universities) reported that approximately 20\% of respondents had no clear career direction when entering university and that approximately 33--41\% would consider choosing a different major. The original survey should be cited before these figures are used as evidence.
    \item MOET Circular 01/2024 includes a 15\% threshold related to student attrition. This quality-assurance threshold should not be presented as the observed national first-year dropout rate or as direct evidence of student--major mismatch.
    \item Employer-reported skills gaps are documented by organizations such as the World Bank, ILO, and Vietnamese labor agencies. The General Statistics Office estimated that 29.2\% of workers held degrees or certificates in 2025.
    \item Reports also describe students choosing fields \textit{theo cảm tính} (based on feelings), peer influence, or expected income rather than on informed self-knowledge and labor-market evidence.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Problems inherent to career orientation as a practice (independent of Vietnam)}

Beyond Vietnam-specific concerns, career orientation faces structural limitations regardless of whether it is provided by a human counselor or an AI-based system:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Assessment tools have important limitations.} Holland's RIASEC framework may oversimplify individuals when its results are reduced to a three-letter code. Research generally finds only modest relationships between person--environment fit and outcomes such as satisfaction, and the framework's cross-cultural validity requires careful evaluation outside the populations in which it was developed. Self-report tools are also vulnerable to social-desirability bias: students may provide answers they consider desirable rather than answers that accurately reflect their views.
    \item \textbf{Career decisions are made under uncertainty.} Labor markets can change faster than school curricula or guidance systems, and AI and automation continue to reshape skill requirements and job roles. No guidance system can predict with certainty which fields will be in demand when today's students graduate from university.
    \item \textbf{Career guidance is under-resourced in many countries.} OECD research based on PISA data identifies substantial career uncertainty among students and misalignment between occupational expectations and labor-market demand. Counselor shortages and unequal access to guidance are therefore broader structural problems rather than issues unique to Vietnam.
    \item \textbf{Teenagers have limited real-world exposure.} Students can report informed interest only in fields they have encountered. Assessments may therefore overlook careers to which a student has not been exposed, even when those careers could be suitable.
    \item \textbf{Family and cultural pressures can outweigh a student's psychological profile.} In some Confucian-influenced East Asian contexts, parental expectations and filial obligations are associated with career decisions, sometimes independently of a student's interests or aptitudes. Thus, even an accurate assessment may not determine the student's eventual choice.
    \item \textbf{Equity and access gaps affect who benefits from guidance.} Rural and lower-income students often have less access to high-quality educational and career guidance than their urban and higher-income peers. This pattern is found in many countries and is not unique to Vietnam.
    \item \textbf{AI-based recommendations introduce additional risks.} Career and job recommenders trained on historical data can reproduce gender and other social biases. Complex models may also provide insufficiently transparent explanations for their recommendations, potentially undermining student trust and making bias more difficult to identify and correct.
\end{itemize}

\section{Pain Points and Proposed Remedies}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pain point:} Existing tools rely almost entirely on self-reported
    questionnaires, with no signal from how a student actually performs on career-relevant
    tasks.\\
    \textbf{Remedy:} Introduce short, simulated micro-tasks per career field and derive a
    fit score from actual task performance, not just self-rating.

    \item \textbf{Pain point:} Most personality/interest frameworks in use are generic and
    international, disconnected from Vietnam's own curriculum and admission system.\\
    \textbf{Remedy:} Ground the platform in the 2018 General Education Program and the
    standard subject combinations (tổ hợp môn), directly mapping them to eligible majors.

    \item \textbf{Pain point:} Labor-market and admission-cutoff data referenced by existing
    tools is static and updated infrequently, quickly going out of date.\\
    \textbf{Remedy:} Use AI-driven scraping/aggregation to keep salary, demand, and
    admission-cutoff data continuously refreshed from public sources.

    \item \textbf{Pain point:} Students cannot easily see the consequence of changing their
    subject combination on which majors and careers remain open to them.\\
    \textbf{Remedy:} Provide a "what-if" simulator that shows, in real time, which majors and
    careers open or close as a student changes their subject combination.

    \item \textbf{Pain point:} Existing solutions are fragmented — a survey here, a career fair
    there — with no single, ongoing journey from assessment to decision.\\
    \textbf{Remedy:} Offer one platform spanning assessment, exploration, pathway planning,
    and mentorship, so guidance persists throughout a student's high school years.
\end{itemize}

\section{Proposed System Features}

The proposed system, Compass AI, is organized around six functional areas.

\subsection{Dynamic Survey \& Academic Engine (Personalization)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Adaptive onboarding survey with branching questions based on prior answers, rather
    than a fixed static form.
    \item Academic profile import — subject grades/transcript per the GDPT 2018 curriculum,
    entered manually or imported from Vietnamese school record formats.
    \item Subject-combination (tổ hợp môn) tracking — mapped to Vietnam's standard combinations
    (A00, A01, B00, C00, D01, D07, etc.) and their eligible university majors.
    \item Real-time "capability portrait" dashboard — a radar chart combining Holland RIASEC
    personality typing, multiple intelligences, and academic strengths.
    \item Periodic re-assessment each semester, with interest-shift tracking to flag significant
    profile changes over time.
    \item Exportable PDF profile report for parents/counselors.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Micro-Task Exploration Loop}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Curated career-field catalog (an initial set of roughly 15–25 fields for the MVP,
    expandable later).
    \item Micro-task library per field — short simulated scenarios (e.g., triaging a mock
    patient, pricing a tour package, debugging a snippet, writing a product pitch).
    \item Multiple task formats — scenario multiple-choice, short written response,
    drag-and-drop simulation, timed challenge.
    \item Performance-based fit scoring, derived from how a student actually performs rather
    than self-rating alone.
    \item Smart task queue that nudges students toward unexplored fields.
    \item Bookmarking of career paths, badges/achievements for exploration breadth, and pattern
    surfacing (e.g., "students like you also excelled at…").
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Visual Pathway \& Market Analytics}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Interactive pathway map — Subject Combination $\rightarrow$ Major $\rightarrow$
    Career, as a node-graph or Sankey-style visualization.
    \item Search/filtering by interest, current subject combination, or region within Vietnam.
    \item Labor-demand and growth-trend charts by sector, based on Vietnamese labor-market
    reports.
    \item University admission cutoff-score (điểm chuẩn) lookup and comparison across
    universities and years, covering both the THPT exam-score and transcript-based
    (xét học bạ) admission routes.
    \item "What-if" simulator showing which majors/careers open or close as a student changes
    subject combination.
    \item Side-by-side career comparison (salary in VND, demand, years of study, personality
    fit).
    \item Personal roadmap builder for saving a multi-year plan, and a job/industry news feed
    for bookmarked or explored careers.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Mentor \& Alumni Insights Space (Community Forum)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Topic-based forum structure organized by field/specialization (e.g., Computer
    Science, DevOps, MLOps, Business, Medicine, Design).
    \item Post-and-reply threads, topic follow/subscribe, and upvoting/helpful-marking of
    replies.
    \item Contributor profiles with role badges (current student, alumni, working
    professional) and field tags.
    \item In-app direct messaging between users, separate from the public forum and subject to
    reporting/moderation given that the user base includes minors.
    \item "A day in my life as X" story library of alumni-submitted posts, and search across
    all forum topics and posts.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cross-Cutting Platform Features}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Multi-role accounts (student or parent, consultant/mentor/alumnus/counselor,
    administrator).
    \item Notifications for survey/task reminders, new mentor activity, and new pathway
    recommendations.
    \item Privacy and data controls, including consent screens and parental visibility into
    activity.
    \item Home dashboard showing profile completeness, recommended next actions, and recent
    activity.
    \item Counselor console with class-wide trend views (e.g., "60\% of Grade 11 leaning
    STEM") and the ability to assign tasks to a whole class.
    \item Unified export/report combining survey results, top pathway recommendations, and
    suggested mentors, shareable with parents.
    \item Global search/filtering across careers, majors, and universities, plus
    platform-wide moderation and reporting tools.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{AI Layer}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Adaptive next-question selection in the survey, starting rule-based with a later
    upgrade path to machine learning.
    \item Fit-score ranking model combining survey and micro-task performance.
    \item Freeform-text topic/thread recommendation, surfacing relevant forum discussions
    based on a student's profile or recent activity.
    \item Career Q\&A chat assistant grounded in the platform's own market data
    (retrieval-augmented generation over the salary/demand dataset).
    \item Disengagement/dropout-risk flagging for counselors.
\end{itemize}

This layer is treated as a bonus scope, to be built last and refined after the other five
areas are in place.

\section{Datasource and Expected Result}

\subsection{Datasource}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Academic and admission data} — the GDPT 2018 curriculum structure, standard
    subject combinations and their mapping to eligible majors, and university admission-cutoff
    scores, sourced from the Ministry of Education and Training (\url{https://moet.gov.vn}) and
    individual university admission portals.
    \item \textbf{Labor-market data} — sector-level labor demand and growth trends from the
    Ministry of Labor, Invalids and Social Affairs and the General Statistics Office
    (\url{https://www.gso.gov.vn}), plus salary and hiring-demand data scraped and aggregated
    from job sites such as VietnamWorks (\url{https://www.vietnamworks.com}) and TopCV
    (\url{https://www.topcv.vn}).
    \item \textbf{Student personal academic data} — grades and subject combinations entered
    directly by students, since no unified format exists across school record systems such as
    VNEdu or SMAS.
    \item \textbf{Platform-generated data} — micro-task content authored by the content team,
    alumni-submitted "day in the life" stories, and forum posts/ratings contributed by users.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Expected Result}

The expected outcome is an AI-powered career orientation platform for Vietnamese high school
students, built around the four pillars above, serving students, parents, counselors, and
alumni/mentors. Compared to existing solutions, the platform is expected to differentiate
itself through performance-based personalization, alignment with Vietnam's own education and
admission system, a what-if subject-combination simulator, and continuously refreshed
labor-market data maintained through automated aggregation rather than manual updates.
